Introduction

There are many excellent ways to access, explore and use geospatial information without being a GIS (Geospatial Information System) professional. Below are a few interactive map viewers and story maps to explore to get you started. This is, by no means, an exhaustive list! In most cases, a help files accompanies the viewer so don't be afraid to skim the help pages for tips and tricks on how to use the viewer.

Printing and exporting maps from web resources can be tricky! Screen grabs to the rescue!

 

Interactive Maps

Connecticut Environmental Conditions Online (CT ECO)

CT ECO is a partnership between UConn CLEAR and CT DEEP. It contains maps as pdfs, map viewers, map and image services and some data download along with a wealth of informational pages and data guides.

 

CT ECO Aerial Imagery Viewer

The CT ECO Aerial Imagery Viewer contains most of Connecticut's digital aerial imagery.

  1. Open the CT ECO Aerial Imagery Viewer.

  2. Click on the Layer List icon. Layer List Help

  3. Turn on and off different layers. Notice the one checked on the top of the list is the visible one.
  4. Zoom into a location you are familiar with - use the Search if you are lost.
  5. Turn layers on and off again. Look for
    1. Differences in season (leaf on and leaf off)
    2. Differences in pixel resolution (3 inch to 1 meter)
    3. Differences in years (change)
If you have time
  1. Click on the Swipe Tool and compare two images. Swipe Tool Help
  2. Use Transparency to compare two images. Look for the ... next to a layer to access the menu containing Transparency.

 

CT ECO Elevation Viewer

The CT ECO Elevation Viewer contains Lidar collected in a 2016 flight. Lidar is laser points collected from a sensor on an airplane that are used to determine elevation. Jump to the 3D Lidar Point Viewer to see the points themselves. Here, only points that hit the bare ground and shown as pixels instead of points. Lidar can reach the ground through trees so artifacts on the ground that are visible. Buildings have been removed from the bare earth elevation data and water has been flattened.

  1. Open the CT ECO Elevation Viewer.

  2. Click on the Layer List icon. Layer List Help

  3. Turn on and off different layers. Notice the one checked on the top of the list is the visible one. The Hillshade, Shaded Relief, Slope and Aspect are different ways to look at the same base layer - Elevation.
  4. Zoom in to a location you are familiar with - use the Search if you are lost.
  5. Turn layers on and off different elevation layers. Look for
    1. Textures - differences between natural areas like wetlands, farm fields, forests, yards and cities.
    2. Slope - steep areas and flat areas.
    3. Turn on the contour lines and compare with an elevation layer (such as hillshade). Contour lines show similar information to the elevation layers but in an entirely different way.
If you have time
  1. Locate stone walls that exist in a forested area. Notice that they are not visible when using imagery alone, but quite visible in the bare earth Lidar elevation.
  2. View the Lidar points themselves int he 3D Lidar Point Viewer. Use the Viewer Help and informational web pages to learn how to move around and what you are looking at.

 

CT ECO Advanced Map Viewer

The CT ECO Advanced Map Viewer is designed for viewing multiple natural resource layers at once.

  1. Open the CT ECO Advanced Map Viewer

  2. Click on the Layers panel at the bottom. Layer List Help

  3. Turn on and off different layers. Notice the one checked on the top of the list is the visible one. Use the + to expand groups. If a layer is grayed out, it is because the group is not checked on OR there is a scale dependency meaning that you need to zoom in or out in order to check the layer on.
  4. Zoom in to a location you are familiar with.
  5. Turn layers on and off different layers.
    1. Depending on your interest, look for water resources, water quality classifications, soils and more.
    2. With one of the thematic layers selected (like water resources or soils), click on one of the map features (a point, line or polygon) to see the attribute information about that feature in a pop-up. Click on a few others.
    3. Display multiple layers at once. For example, (1) display watersheds with water quality, or (2) hydric soils with elevation hillshade, or (3) farmland soils with impervious surfaces (build environment group).
    4. Change the base map. Also notice the imagery and elevation categories at the bottom of the layer list.
If you have time

Explore more layers and layer combinations. This viewer is loaded!

Explore other functions in the Advanced Viewer which are explained on the Advanced Viewer Help page.

 

 

OPTIONAL: Other CT ECO Viewers

Visit the CT ECO Map Viewers page for a complete list. Here are a few that may be of interest.

CT DEEP Fish Community Data - Inland Waters. A map interface for searching DEEP fish and macroinvertebrate community data for inland waters by town, waterbody and species.

Sea Level Rise Effects on Roads and Marshes. View a future scenario of tidal marsh migration and road flooding in the years 2025, 2040, 2055 and 2085.

CT Aquaculture Mapping Atlas. All layers related to Connecticut's Aquaculture resource and industry.

CT MS4 Viewer. Focused on stormwater management across Connecticut.

 

OPTIONAL: Connecticut's Changing Landscape Website and Interactive Viewer

The Connecticut's Changing Landscape is a remote-sensing based land cover study that charts changes in Connecticut land cover over a 30-year period (1985 to 2015). You will learn more about this on Friday.

  1. Go to the Connecticut's Changing Landscape website.
  2. Click on the Connecticut drop-down and go to the Interactive Map. Click Go To Viewer to make it bigger or follow the direct link.
    1. Click on the Layer List icon.
    2. Turn on and off different layers. Notice the one checked on the top of the list is the visible one. Look for
      1. The By Town layers all color the towns based on levels of the different classes and change of that class.
      2. Forest fragmentation layers where the forest class is colored based on forest fragmentation class.
      3. Change maps and land cover maps for the different years.
    3. Zoom in to a location you are familiar with - use the Search if you are lost.
    4. Turn layers on and off different layers. Look for
      1. Land cover classes in an area you are familiar with
      2. Land cover change in an area you are familiar with
      3. Compare different years using the swipe tool
If you have time

Explore the sister Long Island Sound Watershed Land Cover Viewer. This is similar to the Connecticut version, but is by watershed. It also includes impervious surface layers as well as riparian zone land cover.

Explore the Numbers and Charts section of the website where the quantitative land cover statistics by town are displayed in different, interactive ways.

Connecticut's 1934 Aerial Photography, UConn MAGIC

Connecticut was the first state to conduct a statewide flight for the collection of aerial imagery and it was in 1934!

  1. Open the Neighborhood Change in Connecticut, 1934 to Present viewer.
  2. Use the zoom and/or search to to explore areas across the state. Look for
    1. Highways, or lack of
    2. Changes in land cover
    3. Something you are familiar with that existed in 1934.

OPTIONAL: Does Your Town have a GIS?

Most municipalities in Connecticut have an online GIS. Some are created and maintained by the town itself, some are created and maintained by an outside firm and some are created and maintained by the regional council of government. These interactive maps usually contain layers that pertain to a towns operation, such as parcels, land owners and zoning.

  1. Google your town GIS and look for an interactive GIS viewer.
  2. Can you find a place your are familiar with, like where you live or go to school?
  3. Explore other information about your town.

 

Story Maps

Story maps are an excellent way to combine maps, photos, text, videos and more in one place to tell and interactive and interesting story. They are easy to create and an excellent way to present the results of a project.

ArcGIS Story Maps Gallery (using the new Story Map Builder)

  1. Scroll down and find the Nature and environment album.
  2. Click on a story map that interests you. Notice
    1. Texts, graphics and charts
    2. Interactive maps
    3. Interactive "blocks" that scroll down or to the side within the story

Optional: Classic Story Maps Gallery (using "classic" templates)

  1. Scroll down and find the Nature and Conservation category
  2. Click on a story map that interests you. Notice
    1. Texts, graphics and charts
    2. Interactive maps

About NRCA
The Natural Resources Conservation Academy (NRCA) is an innovative program in conservation and land use planning that engages Connecticut high school students, adult conservation volunteers and teachers with conservation efforts at the community level. NRCA’s three interconnected efforts benefit students and adults by conducting education on natural resource management and geospatial technology, and facilitating the use of these new skills in the implementation of local conservation projects that benefit the community.
